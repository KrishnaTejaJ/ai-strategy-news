## 1. China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/china-seeks-a-i-independence-weakening-trumps-leverage/)  

**So What:** China's push for AI independence undermines US leverage in tech competition

China's efforts to develop a domestic AI ecosystem, including alternatives to American chips, reduce the effectiveness of US export controls and increase China's autonomy in the tech sector. This move also signals China's commitment to self-reliance in critical technologies, potentially altering the global tech landscape. As a result, the US may need to reassess its strategy for competing with China in the tech domain.

**Watch For:** Further investments by China in domestic chip production and AI research

## 2. Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/microsoft-google-and-xai-will-let-the-government-test-their-ai-models-before-launch/)  

**So What:** Pre-launch evaluation of AI models by the US government enhances AI governance

The agreement between Microsoft, Google, and xAI to allow the US government to evaluate unreleased AI models for cybersecurity and national security risks marks a significant step towards more effective AI governance. This collaboration can help identify and mitigate potential risks associated with AI systems, ultimately contributing to a safer and more secure AI ecosystem. However, it also raises questions about the balance between innovation and regulation.

**Watch For:** The development of formal guidelines and standards for AI model evaluation and risk assessment
