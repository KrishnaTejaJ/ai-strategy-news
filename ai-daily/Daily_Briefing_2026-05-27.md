## 1. China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/china-seeks-a-i-independence-weakening-trumps-leverage/)  

**So What:** China's acceleration of domestic AI ecosystem development undermines US leverage over China's tech sector.

China's efforts to build a domestic AI ecosystem, including the development of alternatives to American chips, are a direct response to US export controls. This could have significant second-order effects, including reducing China's dependence on US technology and increasing its ability to compete with the US in the global AI market. The success of Chinese companies like DeepSeek and Huawei in developing domestic AI capabilities will be crucial in determining the outcome of this effort.

**Watch For:** Monitoring the progress of Chinese companies in developing domestic AI capabilities and the impact of US export controls on China's tech sector.

## 2. Beyond AI: What the Pentagon is missing with its trimmed ‘critical technologies’ list
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Beyond AI: What the Pentagon is missing with its trimmed ‘critical technologies’ list](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/beyond-ai-what-the-pentagon-is-missing-with-its-trimmed-critical-technologies-list/)  

**So What:** The Pentagon's trimmed list of critical technologies may overlook key areas for investment and innovation in emerging technologies.

The Pentagon's decision to narrow its list of critical technologies may be a positive step towards prioritization, but it also risks overlooking important areas for investment and innovation. The article highlights the need for the Pentagon to improve its prioritization and funding of emerging technologies to address capability gaps and strengthen long-term military innovation. This has significant implications for the US's ability to compete with adversaries like China in the tech domain.

**Watch For:** Future Pentagon budget allocations and investment priorities in emerging technologies, particularly in areas not included on the trimmed list.

## 3. Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/microsoft-google-and-xai-will-let-the-government-test-their-ai-models-before-launch/)  

**So What:** Pre-launch testing of AI models by the US government could improve national security and mitigate cybersecurity risks.

The agreement between Microsoft, Google, and xAI to allow the US government to evaluate unreleased AI models for cybersecurity and national security risks is a significant development in AI governance. This could help identify and mitigate potential risks associated with AI models before they are released to the public, and demonstrates a growing recognition of the need for collaboration between tech companies and governments on AI safety and security.

**Watch For:** The outcomes of the pre-launch testing process and its potential impact on the development of AI governance frameworks and regulations.

## 4. China’s AI optimism isn’t what it seems
**Source:** ChinaTalk  
**Link:** [China’s AI optimism isn’t what it seems](https://www.chinatalk.media/p/chinas-ai-optimism-isnt-what-it-seems)  

**So What:** China's enthusiasm for AI may mask underlying concerns and fears about the technology's impact on the economy and society.

The article suggests that China's optimism about AI may be driven by a mix of genuine enthusiasm and fear about the potential consequences of the technology. This highlights the need for a nuanced understanding of China's AI ambitions and the potential risks and challenges associated with its development and deployment. The article also underscores the importance of considering the social and economic context in which AI is being developed and used in China.

**Watch For:** Signs of growing concern or pushback against AI development in China, particularly among workers and communities that may be most affected by automation and job displacement.

## 5. Rethinking organizational design in the age of agentic AI
**Source:** Artificial intelligence – MIT Technology Review  
**Link:** [Rethinking organizational design in the age of agentic AI](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/05/26/1137584/rethinking-organizational-design-in-the-age-of-agentic-ai/)  

**So What:** The adoption of agentic AI in organizations requires significant changes to operations, infrastructure, and workforce management.

The article highlights the disconnect between ambition and execution in organizations adopting agentic AI, with many citing a lack of readiness across people, processes, and workflows. This underscores the need for organizations to rethink their design and operations to support the integration of AI agents, and to invest in workforce development and training to ensure that employees have the skills needed to work effectively with AI.

**Watch For:** Examples of organizations that have successfully integrated agentic AI and the strategies they used to address the challenges associated with adoption, including workforce development and operational redesign.
