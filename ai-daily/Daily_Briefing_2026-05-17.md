## 1. China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/china-seeks-a-i-independence-weakening-trumps-leverage/)  

**So What:** China's efforts to build a domestic A.I. ecosystem could reduce US leverage over China's tech sector

China's acceleration of domestic A.I. development, particularly in alternatives to American chips, could mitigate the impact of US export controls and reduce dependence on US technology. This move may also have second-order effects on the global semiconductor supply chain, as China seeks to establish itself as a major player. The development of domestic A.I. capabilities could also enhance China's overall tech competitiveness.

**Watch For:** Monitoring China's progress in developing domestic A.I. alternatives and assessing the effectiveness of US export controls in limiting China's access to advanced technologies

## 2. Beyond AI: What the Pentagon is missing with its trimmed ‘critical technologies’ list
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Beyond AI: What the Pentagon is missing with its trimmed ‘critical technologies’ list](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/beyond-ai-what-the-pentagon-is-missing-with-its-trimmed-critical-technologies-list/)  

**So What:** The Pentagon's prioritization of critical technologies may overlook key capability gaps and innovation areas

The Defense Department's decision to narrow its list of critical technologies may be a positive step, but it also risks overlooking important emerging technologies that could address overlooked capability gaps and strengthen long-term military innovation. The Pentagon must improve its prioritization and funding processes to ensure that critical technologies are adequately supported. This could have implications for US tech competitiveness and national security.

**Watch For:** Assessing the Pentagon's updated list of critical technologies and evaluating the impact on US military innovation and competitiveness

## 3. Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/microsoft-google-and-xai-will-let-the-government-test-their-ai-models-before-launch/)  

**So What:** Pre-launch testing of AI models could enhance US government oversight and mitigate cybersecurity risks

The agreements between Microsoft, Google, and xAI to allow pre-launch testing of AI models for cybersecurity and national security risks could significantly improve the US government's ability to identify and mitigate potential threats. This move may also set a precedent for greater cooperation between the tech industry and government on AI governance and risk assessment. However, it also raises questions about the balance between innovation and regulation.

**Watch For:** Evaluating the effectiveness of pre-launch testing in identifying and mitigating AI-related risks, and monitoring the potential impact on AI development and deployment

The concept of a 'latency fund' for economic security, inspired by the idea of 'nuclear latency', could provide a framework for the US to address potential economic security risks and enhance its competitiveness. The idea has implications for US economic security and tech competitiveness, particularly in relation to China.

**Watch For:** Monitoring developments in US economic security policy and assessing the potential impact of a 'latency fund' on US competitiveness and resilience
