## 1. The US military needs a commercial backup plan for an AI chip crisis
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [The US military needs a commercial backup plan for an AI chip crisis](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/the-us-military-needs-a-commercial-backup-plan-for-an-ai-chip-crisis/)  

**So What:** US military readiness is at risk due to potential AI chip supply chain disruptions

The US military's ability to rapidly retrain AI models is critical for battlefield adaptability, and disruptions to AI chip supply chains could significantly impact readiness. This highlights the need for diversified and resilient supply chains, particularly in the context of US-China tech competition. The military's reliance on commercial AI chip suppliers underscores the importance of developing domestic production capabilities or fostering partnerships with allied nations.

**Watch For:** US government initiatives to develop backup plans for AI chip supply chain disruptions

## 3. China’s AI Companies Are Going Closed Source
**Source:** ChinaTalk  
**Link:** [China’s AI Companies Are Going Closed Source](https://www.chinatalk.media/p/chinas-ai-companies-are-going-closed)  

**So What:** China's shift towards closed-source AI development may impact global AI collaboration and intellectual property

China's decision to go closed-source with its AI companies may be driven by concerns over intellectual property protection and national security, but it could also limit international collaboration and the sharing of AI research. This move may further exacerbate the US-China tech competition, as it could lead to a divergence in AI development paths and create new challenges for global AI governance. The implications for semiconductor supply chains and AI chip development are also significant, as Chinese companies may seek to develop proprietary solutions.

**Watch For:** International reactions to China's closed-source AI development strategy and potential impacts on global AI collaboration

## 4. Strategy Without Hubris: How China Rose by Managing America’s Reaction
**Source:** War on the Rocks  
**Link:** [Strategy Without Hubris: How China Rose by Managing America’s Reaction](https://warontherocks.com/strategy-without-hubris-how-china-rose-by-managing-americas-reaction/)  

**So What:** China's strategic management of the US reaction to its rise has implications for US-China tech competition and global governance

China's ability to manage the US reaction to its rise has allowed it to expand its economic and military influence without triggering decisive containment. This strategy has significant implications for the US-China tech competition, as China has been able to exploit the openness of the US economy and tech sector to acquire critical technologies and expertise. The US will need to adapt its strategy to mitigate these risks and promote a more level playing field in the tech sector.

**Watch For:** US policy responses aimed at mitigating China's strategic management of the US reaction, including potential revisions to export control policies and investment screening

## 5. The US and China Need to Talk About Unmanned Nukes
**Source:** The Diplomat  
**Link:** [The US and China Need to Talk About Unmanned Nukes](https://thediplomat.com/2026/04/the-us-and-china-need-to-talk-about-unmanned-nukes/)  

**So What:** The development of unmanned nuclear systems raises critical governance and strategic stability concerns

The development of unmanned nuclear systems, such as autonomous underwater vehicles and hypersonic missiles, raises significant concerns about strategic stability and the potential for unintended escalation. The lack of clear norms and governance frameworks for these systems increases the risk of miscalculation and conflict. US-China dialogue on these issues is essential to establishing common understandings and reducing the risks associated with these emerging technologies.

**Watch For:** US-China diplomatic efforts to establish norms and governance frameworks for unmanned nuclear systems
