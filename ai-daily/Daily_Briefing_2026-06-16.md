## 1. China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/china-seeks-a-i-independence-weakening-trumps-leverage/)  

**So What:** China's push for AI independence undermines US leverage in tech competition

China's efforts to develop a domestic AI ecosystem, including alternatives to American chips, could reduce its reliance on US technology and mitigate the impact of US export controls. This move may also lead to increased investment in China's AI sector, potentially narrowing the gap with US tech companies. As a result, the US may need to reassess its strategy for competing with China in the tech space.

**Watch For:** Implementation and effectiveness of China's domestic AI chip development

## 2. Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/microsoft-google-and-xai-will-let-the-government-test-their-ai-models-before-launch/)  

**So What:** US government gains insight into AI models, potentially informing AI governance policies

The agreements between Microsoft, Google, and xAI to allow the US government to evaluate AI models for cybersecurity and national security risks before launch may lead to more informed AI governance policies. This collaborative approach could also set a precedent for industry-government cooperation on AI risk assessment, potentially influencing the development of AI governance frameworks globally.

**Watch For:** Expansion of this model to other tech companies and potential integration into broader AI regulatory frameworks
