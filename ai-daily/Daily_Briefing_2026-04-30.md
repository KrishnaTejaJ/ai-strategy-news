## 1. ‘Too Dangerous to Release’ Is Becoming AI’s New Normal
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [‘Too Dangerous to Release’ Is Becoming AI’s New Normal](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/too-dangerous-to-release-is-becoming-ais-new-normal/)  

**So What:** Leading AI companies restricting access to capable models due to dual-use risks has significant implications for AI governance and regulation.

This trend highlights the growing recognition of AI's potential risks and the need for more effective governance structures. As AI companies take steps to self-regulate, governments will need to consider how to support or oversee these efforts, potentially leading to new regulations or standards for AI development and deployment. This could also create challenges for researchers and developers relying on open access to AI models.

**Watch For:** Government responses to AI companies' self-regulation, particularly in areas like cybersecurity and biological research.

## 3. China-Proofing the American Industrial Base
**Source:** ChinaTalk  
**Link:** [China-Proofing the American Industrial Base](https://www.chinatalk.media/p/china-proofing-the-american-industrial)  

**So What:** Efforts to 'China-proof' the American industrial base reflect growing concerns about US-China economic interdependence and the need for resilient supply chains.

This trend highlights the increasing focus on economic security and the need for the US to reduce its dependence on Chinese supply chains. As the US seeks to 'China-proof' its industrial base, it will need to invest in domestic manufacturing capabilities, potentially leading to new opportunities for innovation and job creation. However, this could also lead to increased tensions with China and potentially disrupt global trade flows.

**Watch For:** US policies and initiatives aimed at reducing dependence on Chinese supply chains, particularly in critical sectors like semiconductors and pharmaceuticals.
