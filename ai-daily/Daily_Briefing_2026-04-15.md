## 1. Mapping the AI Governance Landscape: April 2026 Update
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Mapping the AI Governance Landscape: April 2026 Update](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/mapping-the-ai-governance-landscape-april-2026-update/)  

**So What:** Rapidly expanding AI governance documents reflect growing concerns over AI risks, but their effectiveness remains unclear.

The increasing number of AI governance documents indicates a rising awareness of AI-related risks and the need for mitigation strategies. However, the actual coverage of these documents in terms of risks, actors, and industry sectors is crucial for understanding their potential impact. The development of taxonomies to categorize these documents will facilitate a more comprehensive understanding of the AI governance landscape.

## 2. AI & Tech Brief: Congress’s crackdown on global chip equipment
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [AI & Tech Brief: Congress’s crackdown on global chip equipment](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/ai-tech-brief-congresss-crackdown-on-global-chip-equipment/)  

**So What:** US legislation to expand export controls on semiconductor manufacturing equipment could significantly impact the US-China tech competition.

The proposed legislation aims to restrict China's access to advanced semiconductor technology, which could hinder its ability to develop and manufacture high-performance chips. This move may escalate the US-China tech competition, potentially leading to retaliatory measures from China. The outcome will depend on the willingness of allied countries to align with US chip restrictions.

**Watch For:** Reactions from China and other affected countries to the proposed legislation, as well as the potential consequences for the global semiconductor supply chain.

## 3. The US military needs a commercial backup plan for an AI chip crisis
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [The US military needs a commercial backup plan for an AI chip crisis](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/the-us-military-needs-a-commercial-backup-plan-for-an-ai-chip-crisis/)  

**So What:** The US military's reliance on AI chips for battlefield adaptability highlights the need for a commercial backup plan to mitigate potential supply chain disruptions.

The ability to rapidly retrain AI models in crisis situations is critical for US military readiness, and the current reliance on specialized AI chips poses a significant risk. Developing a commercial backup plan could ensure continued access to necessary technology, even in the event of a supply chain disruption. This would require collaboration between the US military and commercial chip manufacturers.

**Watch For:** Initiatives by the US military to develop partnerships with commercial chip manufacturers and invest in backup plans for AI chip supply chain disruptions.

## 4. The Bromine Chokepoint: How Strife in the Middle East Could Halt Production of the World’s Memory Chips
**Source:** War on the Rocks  
**Link:** [The Bromine Chokepoint: How Strife in the Middle East Could Halt Production of the World’s Memory Chips](https://warontherocks.com/the-bromine-chokepoint-how-strife-in-the-middle-east-could-halt-production-of-the-worlds-memory-chips/)  

**So What:** Disruptions to bromine supply chains due to conflict in the Middle East could have significant implications for global semiconductor manufacturing.

Bromine is a critical component in the production of semiconductor-grade hydrogen bromide gas, which is used in the manufacturing of memory chips. A disruption to bromine supply chains could have far-reaching consequences for the global semiconductor industry, particularly for South Korean manufacturers that rely heavily on this chemical. This highlights the need for diversified and resilient supply chains.

**Watch For:** Developments in the conflict in the Middle East and their potential impact on bromine supply chains, as well as efforts by semiconductor manufacturers to diversify their supply chains.

## 5. Mutually Automated Destruction: The Escalating Global A.I. Arms Race
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Mutually Automated Destruction: The Escalating Global A.I. Arms Race](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/mutually-automated-destruction-the-escalating-global-a-i-arms-race/)  

**So What:** The accelerating global development of AI-powered autonomous weapons poses significant risks to international security and stability.

The integration of AI-powered autonomous weapons into modern warfare increases the potential for unintended escalation and decreases the ability to control the actions of these systems. This development is driven by the perceived need for a strategic advantage, but it may ultimately lead to a destabilization of the global security landscape. International cooperation and agreements on the development and use of autonomous weapons are critical to mitigating these risks.

**Watch For:** International responses to the development of AI-powered autonomous weapons, including potential agreements or regulations to govern their use and development.
