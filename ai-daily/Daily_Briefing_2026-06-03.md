## 1. China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [China Seeks A.I. Independence, Weakening Trump’s Leverage](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/china-seeks-a-i-independence-weakening-trumps-leverage/)  

**So What:** China's push for AI independence undermines US leverage in tech competition

China's efforts to build a domestic AI ecosystem, including developing alternatives to American chips, reduce US influence over China's tech sector. This move also has second-order effects, such as limiting the efficacy of US export controls and bolstering China's position in the global tech landscape. As a result, the US may need to reassess its strategy for competing with China in the tech domain.

**Watch For:** Further investments by China in domestic chip production and AI research

## 2. Beyond AI: What the Pentagon is missing with its trimmed ‘critical technologies’ list
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Beyond AI: What the Pentagon is missing with its trimmed ‘critical technologies’ list](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/beyond-ai-what-the-pentagon-is-missing-with-its-trimmed-critical-technologies-list/)  

**So What:** Pentagon's narrowed critical technologies list may overlook key innovation areas

The Pentagon's decision to trim its list of critical technologies, while a positive step towards focus, may inadvertently overlook crucial emerging technologies and capability gaps. This could hinder long-term military innovation and leave the US vulnerable to technological surprises. The Pentagon must balance prioritization with a nuanced understanding of the evolving tech landscape.

**Watch For:** Pentagon's future funding allocations for emerging technologies and potential revisions to the critical technologies list

## 3. Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch
**Source:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology  
**Link:** [Microsoft, Google and xAI will let the government test their AI models before launch](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/microsoft-google-and-xai-will-let-the-government-test-their-ai-models-before-launch/)  

**So What:** Pre-launch AI model evaluation enhances US national security and cybersecurity

The agreements between Microsoft, Google, and xAI to allow pre-launch evaluations of AI models for national security and cybersecurity risks mark a significant step towards mitigating potential threats from AI. This collaboration demonstrates a growing recognition of the need for public-private partnerships in AI governance and may set a precedent for future industry-government cooperation.

**Watch For:** Expansion of this evaluation framework to other tech companies and potential establishment of formal AI model review processes

## 4. Can Section 301 effectively replace IEEPA? That is the $166 billion question
**Source:** Atlantic Council  
**Link:** [Can Section 301 effectively replace IEEPA? That is the $166 billion question](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/econographics/can-section-301-effectively-replace-ieepa-that-is-the-166-billion-question/)  

**So What:** Proposed tariff changes may impact US-China trade and tech competition dynamics

The potential replacement of IEEPA with Section 301 as a basis for tariffs could significantly alter the US approach to trade with China, particularly with regards to tech imports. This shift may have far-reaching implications for US-China trade relations, the global semiconductor supply chain, and the ongoing tech competition between the two nations.

**Watch For:** Outcome of the proposed tariff changes and potential responses from China and other affected economies
